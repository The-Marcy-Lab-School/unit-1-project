Mission Statement

Reproductive justice for black women is important because it disportionately impacts black women in comparison to their white counterparts. At the end of the day, the ability to get correct and fair medical treatment and have the choice to make decisions about their bodies is a basic human right that many black women do not have access to.
This issue has and will continue to impact black women and black generations if nothing is done to reform the quality of reproductive rights of black women.

History

Research has shown that unconscious attitudes and stereotypes held by physicians—a form of individual racism—have led to disparate treatment and recommendations for Black patients, such as delayed reproductive health screenings. Furthermore, a history of medical experimentation, coercion, and mistreatment of Black women has fueled a mistrust of the medical system—one that shapes Black communities’ engagement and satisfaction with medical care

The Reality

Structural racism has been seen in policies that segregate Black populations into under resourced neighborhoods, poorly funded schools, and segmented labor markets. These conditions have resulted in systematically higher concentrations of poverty, unemployment, and lower-paying jobs and greater dependence on social services in the Black community. Twenty-four percent of Black women are dependent on Title X programs, which provide reproductive health care services for low-income women and close to a third of Black women of reproductive age (15–44) are enrolled in Medicaid.

The Affect

Black women are about four times more likely than white women to die in pregnancy, and their babies are two to three times more likely than white babies to die within the first year.

Maternal Mortality Rate

In 2020, the maternal mortality rate for non-Hispanic Black women was 55.3 deaths per 100,000 live births, 2.9 times the rate for non-Hispanic White women.

Abortion Banned

The abortion banned nationwide would lead to a 21% increase in the number of pregnancy-related deaths for all women and a 33% increase among Black women.
