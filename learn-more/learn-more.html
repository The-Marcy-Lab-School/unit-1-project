Contraceptives and Barrier Methods

It's important for everyone to know what forms of contraceptives, also known as birth control, are available to them. Some methods are more effective at preventing pregnancy, while other methods, known as barriers, are more protective against sexually transmitted diseases (STIs). Below, you'll find some information on all available methods and lifestyles that you should know safely practice sex in a way that's best for you.

IUD

An IUD (intrauterine device) is inserted directly into the uterus by a medical professional. The hormonal type can last up to 3-5 years, and the copper type can last up to 10 years. The hormonal type releases hormones to prevent pregnancy, while copper is a natural spermicide and will kill any sperm that may enter the uterus.

Effectiveness: 99%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $1,300

Usage: Lasts 3-10 years

Birth Control Implant

The implant is inserted into the upper arm by a medical professional, which releases hormones to prevent pregnancy.

Effectiveness: 99%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $1,300

Usage: Lasts up to 5 years

Birth Control Shot

The shot is not suitable for long-term use (2+ years) unless no other methods are viable. If more than 2 weeks go by without getting a new shot, you may be advised to take a pregnancy test if you've been sexually active within that timeframe.

Effectiveness: 96%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $150

Usage: Lasts 3 months

Birth Control Ring

The ring is inserted into the vagina and releases hormones to prevent pregnancy. A prescription is needed to get the Ring.

Effectiveness: 93%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $200

Usage: Insert and remove once a month.

Birth Control Patch

The patch can be worn on certain parts of the body, and releases hormones through the skin. A prescription is needed to get the Patch.

Effectiveness: 93%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $150

Usage: Replace weekly

Birth Control Pill

The pill uses hormones to prevent pregnancy. A prescription might be needed to get the Pill.

Effectiveness: 93%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $50

Usage: Take daily

Condom

Condoms are worn on the penis and must be replaced with every use. Condoms are made from latex, plastic, or lambskin. Oil-based lubricants must not be used with latex, as it damages the rubber. Lambskin condoms are less effective at preventing STIs since they contain tiny holes, which are small enough to block sperm but big enough to let bacteria through. Condoms can be obtained for free from some health centers, and can be purchased online or in drugstores in packs of 3 for $2 to $6. Packs of 12 or more typically are less than $1 each.

Effectiveness: 87%

Prevents STIs: Yes

Cost: $0 to $2 for one

Usage: Use every time

Internal Condom

Internal condoms are also known as "female" condoms, but can be used by either gender. The closed side is inserted into the vagina or anus. Internal condoms are made of a nitrile (soft plastic) outer ring and polyurethane inner ring, with the insides and outsides having a silicone-based lubricant. They can be obtained for free from some health centers, or are purchasable online or in drugstores for $2 to $3.

Effectiveness: 79%

Prevents STIs: Yes

Cost: $0 to $3 for one

Usage: Use every time

Diaphragm

The Diaphragm is a shallow, bendable cup that's inserted into the vagina. It covers the cervix and must be used with spermicide. The diaphragm can keep spermicide in place near the cervix. Diaphragms come in different sizes, so a medical professional can fit one for you and provide a prescription. They can also be purchased in drugstores.

Effectiveness: 87%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $75

Usage: Use every time

Birth Control Sponge

The sponge is inserted into the vagina close to the cervix and is filled with spermicide. The sponge has a small, fabric loop that makes it easier to remove. The sponge can be found in most drugstores and online.

Effectiveness: 78-86%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $15

Usage: Use every time

Spermicide

Spermicide is a chemical-based cream or gel that can be inserted directly into the vagina and/or placed on condoms. Spermicide can be obtained over-the-counter in most drugstores, pharmacies, and superstores where condoms are located.

Effectiveness: 78-86%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $270

Usage: Use every time

Cervical Cap

The cervical cap is a small cup made of soft silicone that covers the cervix. It must also be used with spermicide. Cervical caps come in different shapes and sizes, so a medical professional can fit one for you and provide a prescription. They can also be purchased in drugstores.

Effectiveness: 71-86%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $90

Usage:

Fertility Awareness Method (FAM)

There are 3 variations to this method, which are the temperature, cervical mucous, and calendar methods. This is most effective when you use all three methods together, which is known as the symptothermal method. While this can be used as a method of contraception, it's more effective when trying to get pregnant rather than avoid it.

Effectiveness: 77-98%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $20

Usage: Use daily

Withdrawal/Pull Out Method

Withdrawal is when the penis is pulled out of the vagina before ejaculation. Ejaculation must take place away from the vagina and vulva, or there may be a risk of pregnancy. Withdrawal works best when used with additional birth control methods, such as a condom.

Effectiveness: 78%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0

Usage: Use every time

Breastfeeding as Birth Control/ Lactational Amenorrhea (LAM)

By exclusively breastfeeding for the first 6 months after a baby is born, a woman can stop ovulation and her period naturally. For this to be effective, she must nurse your baby every 4-5 hours, and every 6 hours at night. This method is called LAM for "lactational" referring to breastfeeding, and "amenorrhea" referring to not having a period. This is only effective for the first 6 months where a mother breastfeeds.

Effectiveness: 98%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0

Usage: Do every 4-6 hours

Outercourse and Abstinence

Abstinence is not having sex, while outercourse is other sexual activities aside from vaginal sex.

Effectiveness: 100%

Prevents STIs: Yes

Cost: $0

Usage: Use every time

Sterilization/Tubal Ligation/Tube Tying

There are three types of sterilization, which are tubal ligation, bilateral salpingectomy, and Essure sterilization. Tubal ligation is a surgical procedure that permanently cuts, closes, or removes parts of the fallopian tubes. Bilateral salpingectomy is a surgical procedure that removes the fallopian tubes entirely. Essure sterilization is a small coil that a medical professional inserts into the fallopian tubes to block them, but is no longer available in the United States.

Effectiveness: 99%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $6,000

Usage: Lasts for life

Vasectomy

There are two types of vasectomies, which are the incision and no-scalpel (no-cut) methods. The small tubes in the scrotum that can carry semen are cut or blocked off in order to prevent pregnancy. The no-cut method is less likely to get infected or have any complications, and generally takes less time to heal. Vasectomies are usually not reversable.

Effectiveness: 99%

Prevents STIs: No

Cost: $0 to $1000

Usage: Lasts for life
